\begin{abstract}
Financial event models often collapse two distinct problems into one representation:
ordinary market dynamics and the incremental impact of a newly disclosed event. We propose the
Counterfactual Event Bottleneck Transformer (CEBT), an architecture that predicts a no-event
baseline from pre-event market history and routes disclosure information through a compact
stochastic bottleneck that predicts an event-induced residual. The model is trained with matched
same-ticker no-event controls, a residual-suppression loss for controls, and strict temporal
feature gates. On a real public-data experiment covering 7,236 SEC 8-K events from 98 usable
large U.S. firms between 2020 and 2025, full CEBT does not dominate every baseline on raw
regression error. Its evidence is narrower but more interesting: CEBT is the only tested model
with positive held-out abnormal-return rank IC under iid, ticker-clustered, and month-clustered
bootstrap intervals. In paired rank-IC comparisons, CEBT significantly improves over no-event
dynamics and concat fusion, while its improvement over the no-control CEBT ablation is positive
but not statistically resolved. CEBT also suppresses residual magnitude assigned to no-event
controls. The main claim is not that CEBT is a deployable trading system, but that an explicit
event bottleneck and no-event control objective produce a more temporally disciplined
event-residual representation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Disclosure events are difficult modeling targets because market movement after a filing is
not caused only by the filing. A stock's subsequent return reflects market drift, sector movement,
liquidity, firm-specific momentum, and the information content of the disclosure itself. Standard
fusion models mix these sources into one hidden representation, making it hard to tell whether
the model learned an event effect or simply reproduced ordinary dynamics.

We introduce CEBT, an event modeling architecture that decomposes the forecast into
\[
    \hat{y}_{t+h} = f_{\mathrm{no\ event}}(x_{<t}) + g_{\mathrm{event}}(z_e, x_{<t}),
\]
where \(f_{\mathrm{no\ event}}\) is learned from pre-event numeric history and \(z_e\) is a compact
stochastic disclosure bottleneck. The bottleneck is trained with matched no-event controls:
dates for the same ticker that are far from known SEC 8-K events. For controls, the model is
penalized when it assigns a large event residual. This makes the residual interpretable as a
candidate event-specific increment rather than an unconstrained shortcut.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A no-event-plus-residual neural architecture for financial disclosure modeling.
    \item A stochastic event bottleneck trained with matched no-event controls.
    \item A leakage-safe public-data implementation using SEC accepted timestamps and public
    price histories.
    \item A real-data study showing that residual control changes model behavior and that CEBT
    produces the strongest abnormal-return rank ordering under clustered uncertainty checks.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

CEBT is closest to causal and event-driven financial forecasting. Classical event-study methods
estimate abnormal returns around public information releases \citep{fama1969adjustment,
brown1985eventstudies,mackinlay1997eventstudies}; CEBT keeps this event-study grounding but
learns a neural decomposition of baseline dynamics and disclosure residuals. CAMEF studies
causal-augmented multi-modal financial forecasting \citep{chen2025camef}. Causal Transformer and
G-Transformer estimate counterfactual outcomes in temporal settings \citep{melnychuk2022causaltransformer,
feng2024gtransformer}. The architectural ingredients also connect to Transformers
\citep{vaswani2017attention}, variational latent-variable models \citep{kingma2013autoencoding},
and the information bottleneck principle \citep{tishby2000information}. Financial-text work shows
that disclosure language contains useful signal but is easy to confound with disclosure timing,
firm identity, and market state \citep{loughran2011liability,cohen2020lazy,araci2019finbert}.
Time-series foundation models such as Chronos, Time-LLM, MOMENT, and Mamba improve sequence
modeling capacity but do not isolate disclosure-induced residuals
\citep{ansari2024chronos,jin2023timellm,wu2024moment,gu2023mamba}. Financial LLM benchmarks and
agents test retrieval or question-answering over filings rather than a new event-residual
architecture \citep{islam2023financebench,finrate2026}. Leakage-focused work such as Profit
Mirage motivates strict temporal evaluation in financial agents \citep{li2025profitmirage}.
Subliminal-learning results further motivate caution around hidden outcome signal in generated or
compressed representations \citep{cloud2025subliminal}.

\section{Method}

\paragraph{Notation and estimand.}
Each sample \(i\) has a label start date \(\tau_i\), a 40-trading-day pre-event tensor
\(X_i \in \mathbb{R}^{40 \times 8}\), an event embedding \(e_i \in \mathbb{R}^{256}\), metadata
\(m_i \in \mathbb{R}^{6}\), a control indicator \(c_i\), and a target vector
\(y_i \in \mathbb{R}^{3}\). The three targets are five-trading-day market-adjusted return,
volatility jump, and volume jump. CEBT estimates a predictive event residual,
not an identified structural treatment effect. The counterfactual claim is therefore modest:
matched no-event rows define a placebo pressure that encourages the residual channel to be small
when no disclosure is present, while real causal identification would require stronger
assumptions and intraday or quasi-experimental evidence.

\paragraph{NoEventDynamics.}
The no-event module receives a fixed pre-event window of price, volume, market, and relative-return
features. A Transformer encoder pools this history and predicts the ordinary forward movement
expected without relying on the event text.

\paragraph{EventBottleneck.}
The event module embeds the disclosure text and metadata, then parameterizes a Gaussian latent
\(q_\phi(z_i \mid e_i,m_i)=\mathcal{N}(\mu_i,\mathrm{diag}(\exp(\ell_i)))\). During training,
\(z_i=\mu_i+\epsilon\exp(\ell_i/2)\), \(\epsilon\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)\); at evaluation, we use
\(\mu_i\). The prior is \(p(z)=\mathcal{N}(0,I)\). The latent dimension is intentionally small:
the paper-scale model uses eight latent coordinates, hidden width 96, two Transformer encoder
layers, four attention heads, and dropout 0.10. We add a KL term and sparsity term so the event
representation cannot behave like an unrestricted text embedding.

\paragraph{ResidualOutcomeHead.}
The residual head predicts an event-induced delta from the no-event state, metadata, and
\(z_i\). The final forecast is the sum of the no-event forecast and this residual:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{y}_i = b_\theta(X_i,m_i) + \Delta_\theta(s_i,z_i,m_i),
    \label{eq:decomposition}
\end{equation}
where \(s_i\) is the pooled no-event state.

\paragraph{Training objective.}
The loss combines supervised MSE, a no-event control residual penalty, KL regularization,
latent sparsity, and a small residual-variance consistency penalty:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} =
\lambda_y \lVert \hat{y} - y \rVert_2^2
+ \lambda_c \mathbb{1}_{control}\lVert \Delta_e \rVert_2^2
+ \lambda_{kl}D_{KL}(q(z_e)\|p(z))
+ \lambda_s\lVert z_e\rVert_1
+ \lambda_v\mathrm{Var}(\Delta_e).
\label{eq:loss}
\end{equation}
For the paper-scale run, \(\lambda_y=1.0\), \(\lambda_c=0.25\), \(\lambda_{kl}=0.01\),
\(\lambda_s=0.001\), and \(\lambda_v=0.05\). The control penalty is averaged over no-event rows
in each batch. The consistency term is the batch variance of the residual output and is included
only as a small stabilizer, not as the main source of the result.

\section{Data and Leakage Controls}

We build a public-data experiment from SEC EDGAR APIs \citep{secapi}. The paper-scale run covers
98 usable large U.S. firms, SEC 8-K filings from 2020--2025, and daily public prices. Tickers are
mapped to CIKs through the SEC company-tickers file. Filings are read from SEC submissions JSON,
including archived submissions, using the SEC \texttt{acceptanceDateTime} field and the primary
document URL in the EDGAR archive. The text parser removes scripts, styles, and markup with
BeautifulSoup and stores the extracted text plus SHA-256 provenance. Each event row stores CIK,
ticker, accession number, form type, filing date, accepted timestamp, available timestamp, source
URL, text hash, and local provenance.

Daily prices are downloaded from Stooq first and Yahoo's public chart endpoint as a fallback.
SPY is the market proxy for abnormal returns. The trading calendar is inferred from observed price
tables rather than from an invented holiday file. Event label windows start from the first tradable
session after public availability. If a filing is accepted after 16:00 ET, or on a non-trading
day, the label starts on the next observed trading day.

Features use only prices strictly before the event label start date. The eight numeric features
are one-day return, five-day compounded return, 20-day compounded return, 20-day realized
volatility, 20-day volume z-score, market return, market-relative return, and close-to-open return.
Labels use future returns only as targets. We construct one deterministic same-ticker no-event
control for each filing, excluding dates within a 10-calendar-day blackout window around known
events. Control rows receive a zero event embedding and explicit control metadata; this makes the
control objective clean for a first test, but it is also a limitation discussed below.

\section{Experiments}

The paper-scale run uses \texttt{configs/paper\_v3.yaml} with seed 29. Rows with
\(\tau_i \leq\) 2023-12-31 are training rows, rows with \(\tau_i \leq\) 2024-12-31 are validation
rows, and later rows are held out. This gives 9,729 training rows, 2,280 validation rows, and
2,463 held-out rows across 98 held-out tickers and 13 held-out label-start months. All models are
trained for 15 epochs with AdamW, learning rate \(10^{-3}\), weight decay \(10^{-4}\), batch size
64, and mixed precision on CUDA when available. No early stopping or hyperparameter search is used
for the reported run.

We compare five models: no-event dynamics only, text-only MLP, concat fusion, CEBT without
control losses, and full CEBT. The no-event baseline sees \(X_i\) and metadata but no disclosure
embedding. The text-only baseline sees \(e_i\) and metadata but no price history. Concat fusion
uses the same no-event encoder as CEBT, then directly concatenates the pooled state, event
embedding, and metadata without a stochastic bottleneck or additive residual constraint. CEBT
without controls has the same architecture as full CEBT but sets \(\lambda_c=0\).

Rank IC is Spearman correlation between predicted and realized abnormal return over held-out rows.
The main rank-IC value is computed across all held-out rows; Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap} additionally
shows monthly event-only rank IC buckets with at least eight disclosures. Confidence intervals use
1,000 percentile bootstrap resamples. Row bootstraps sample held-out rows with replacement.
Ticker-clustered and month-clustered intervals sample clusters with replacement and concatenate
all rows in sampled clusters. Paired intervals resample aligned prediction rows and report the
difference in the relevant metric.

\section{Findings}

\paragraph{CEBT is an event-ranking model, not the best raw regressor.}
As shown in Table~\ref{tab:main-results} and Figure~\ref{fig:mse}, concat fusion has the best
point-estimate MSE, 0.0597, while full CEBT has MSE 0.0660. This matters: the architectural
decomposition does not automatically minimize multi-target regression error. The evidence for CEBT
is instead concentrated in rank ordering of event outcomes.

\paragraph{The event-ranking signal survives clustered uncertainty checks.}
Full CEBT has abnormal-return rank IC 0.0463 in Table~\ref{tab:main-results}. Its iid bootstrap
interval is [0.0074, 0.0836], the ticker-clustered interval is [0.0052, 0.0893], and the
month-clustered interval is [0.0112, 0.0768]. No other tested model has a positive clustered
interval excluding zero. Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap} shows that this signal is not a single
company-month artifact, although it is still modest in magnitude.

\paragraph{Paired rank comparisons separate CEBT from stronger baselines.}
CEBT improves paired rank IC over concat fusion by 0.0645 with a 95\% paired interval
[0.0058, 0.1215], and over no-event dynamics by 0.0874 with interval [0.0251, 0.1534]. The
improvement over CEBT without controls is positive, 0.0184, but not resolved by the paired
interval [-0.0202, 0.0595].

\paragraph{Control losses alter the residual semantics.}
The full CEBT model assigns mean absolute event residual 0.0313 to true events and 0.0103 to
controls. The no-control variant assigns 0.0396 to true events and 0.0298 to controls. Thus, the
control objective reduces spurious residual assignment to no-event dates by about 66\%, while
preserving a roughly 3.0x event-to-control residual ratio. Figures~\ref{fig:mosaic} and
\ref{fig:residuals} show the same behavior visually: residual mass is sparse across
company-months and shifted away from placebo rows.

\section{Reproducibility}

The reported run was produced with \texttt{configs/paper\_v3.yaml} and
\texttt{configs/model/cebt\_small.yaml}. The pipeline order is:
\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lllll}
\texttt{10\_build\_events} &
\texttt{20\_download\_prices} &
\texttt{30\_build\_features} &
\texttt{60\_ablate} &
\texttt{70\_make\_tables}
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
The H200 run used PyTorch 2.11.0 with CUDA 12.8. The code stores raw SEC metadata, text hashes,
price rows, feature metadata, model checkpoints, predictions, table CSVs, and generated figures.
The automated tests check timestamp gating, after-hours label starts, feature leakage rejection,
control construction, deterministic cached tensors, finite forward losses, and evaluation leakage
guards. No paid data, closed-source model endpoint, or generated label is used for the main
result.

\section{Limitations}

This is a first paper-scale run, not a final investment model. The text encoder is a deterministic
open hashing baseline rather than a large pretrained financial encoder. Control rows use zero event
embeddings and explicit control metadata, so residual suppression on controls is not yet a hard
placebo-text test. The public price source is not CRSP, and labels use daily closes rather than
intraday event windows. The universe is large-cap biased and may contain survivorship effects,
ticker-history issues, and 8-K item-mix sensitivity. The experiment uses SPY-adjusted returns
rather than richer factor-model residuals and excludes transaction costs. Two configured tickers
did not yield final usable event rows because of SEC retrieval failures during the build. The
counterfactual language should be read as architecture and regularization, not full causal
identification. The next version should test pretrained open embeddings, 10-Q/10-K extensions,
sector and factor controls, and harder placebo controls where no-event dates receive unrelated
historical disclosure text instead of a zero event embedding.

\section{Ethics and Use}

This paper studies event representations using public filings and public daily prices. It is not a
trading recommendation system, and the reported rank signal is too small and too coarsely measured
to justify deployment. The release should include code, configs, and provenance metadata so that
future users can audit leakage and reproduce or falsify the claims.

\section{Conclusion}

CEBT reframes event-driven financial modeling as a decomposition problem: ordinary market dynamics
plus a disclosure-induced residual. In a real public-data experiment, CEBT does not beat concat
fusion on raw MSE, but it produces the strongest abnormal-return rank ordering and remains positive
under ticker- and month-clustered uncertainty checks. The result supports CEBT as a promising
architecture for studying event-specific effects under strict temporal information boundaries.
